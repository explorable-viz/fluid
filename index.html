fluid:
functional language for understanding and interacting with dependencies

Digital technology is transforming the way scientists generate, evaluate and communicate knowledge. Fluid is a functional programming language which makes computational artefacts such as charts, figures and statistical analyses more transparent and explorable. A Fluid chart is written in a purely functional style, but the resulting graphics automatically provide affordances that allow a reader to discover how views relate to each other, and to the underlying code and datasets. The current prototype allows us to link charts to other charts, so that the reader can select data elements and have the relevant parts of the chart be automatically highlighted, and vice versa. See the example below (best viewed in Chrome):

The left-hand chart shows total renewable energy output for the four main kinds of renewable energy (bio, hydro, solar and wind) for three of the leading countries in 2015. The right-hand chart shows a fluctuation in renewables output over time for the same four energy types, just for China.

Visualisation libraries such as Bokeh and spatial analytics applications like GeoDa provide limited support for this kind of linking, but usually only for specific predefined visualisations, or with a certain amount of programmer effort. Fluid takes a more foundational approach, through a runtime that makes linking automatic, application-agnostic and mathematically robust.

To see the Fluid code for the above charts, open the Wrattler notebook in Binder, and then open the fluid-example.wrattler notebook. (Warning: Binder is slow; Wrattler will take several minutes to launch even from a prebuilt image.) Fluid is open source and is available on GitHub.
